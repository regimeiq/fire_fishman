# Limitations

## Interpretation

This project is an observational baseball analytics case study. It can identify public-data patterns, estimate measurable value, and compare outcomes across cohorts. It cannot observe internal player-development plans, private tracking data, medical context, clubhouse information, or proprietary team models.

## Prospect Cohort Size

The prospect cohort contains 43 players across 21 organizations. That is enough to describe patterns and generate hypotheses, but not enough to make broad causal claims about entire organizations. Organization-level rates are especially sensitive to small samples.

## Outcome Labels

Prospect outcomes are broad categories based on early MLB production. They simplify player careers into star, solid, disappointing, and bust buckets. Those labels are useful for analysis, but they can change as players develop.

## MiLB and Pre-Debut Samples

MiLB and pre-debut pitch-level samples are smaller than MLB samples. They are useful for directional comparisons, especially when combined with public MiLB batting records, but they should not be treated as complete measures of a player's minor league skill set.

## Health and Role Changes

Injuries, playing-time changes, swing adjustments, and role changes can interrupt or alter a player evaluation. Dominguez's May 2026 shoulder injury and Volpe's reported swing-plane adjustments are examples of live-context issues that should pause or qualify interpretation.

## Ben Rice 2026 Update

Rice's 2025 breakout and strong early-2026 public-data sample strengthen the readiness-gate framework, but 2026 remains an in-progress season. Final claims about his 2026 performance should be revisited after the full season.

## Team-Level Composite

The pressure/baserunning/defense composite is exploratory. Same-year relationships can overstate usefulness because the metric and team WAR are observed in the same season. The year-ahead correlation is the stricter validation check, and it is more modest.

## Public Data Constraints

Statcast and FanGraphs provide rich public data, but they do not include every input available to MLB teams. The analysis should be read as an external audit of public evidence, not a reconstruction of internal decision-making.
